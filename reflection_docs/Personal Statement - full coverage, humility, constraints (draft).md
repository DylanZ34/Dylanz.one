# Personal Statement Draft (full coverage + humility + constraints)

## Draft v0.1

The moment that made Quizbowl make sense to me wasn’t a buzzer question. It was the whiteboard.

If you only watch a match, it looks like the goal is to know more facts than the other team. But the real work happens earlier, when we map the terrain—history, literature, science—and decide how to become something larger than any one person. Someone takes European history. Someone takes biology. Someone else takes literature. The goal isn’t to be individually impressive; it’s to build a system we can rely on.

We call it “total coverage.” In the ideal version, no question lands in empty space.

I’ve started to realize that “coverage” is my default way of thinking: I like building systems that hold, respecting the unknowns they can’t yet explain, and iterating inside constraints until they become a design space.

That mindset shows up in music first.

When I started composing a piano concerto, I assumed the hard part would be writing a beautiful melody. I was wrong. The hard part is making the melody survivable—giving it structure strong enough to carry it. Orchestration turns inspiration into engineering: if the strings carry the harmony, can a flute answer without disappearing? If I add clarinets for warmth, what changes when the oboes take over later? The sound I want isn’t located in any single line; it emerges from how parts weave together.

I build the piece the way my team builds coverage: section by section, over days. Most days I start with a main melody, orchestrate a short passage, and stop. The next day I return and extend it—an introduction, a development, a conclusion—until separate ideas begin behaving like one work. Then I revise. I draft, listen, edit, and sometimes move an entire passage because it belongs somewhere else. Constraints don’t disappear; they just shift. When I treat them as real, the piece gets better.

The same three-part pattern—structure, humility, constraints—also shapes how I learn.

When I wrote a research paper on quantum computing, I couldn’t learn what I needed in one day. Progress came from reading across multiple days and narrowing toward a research goal. I prefer building up a hypothesis only after I’ve learned enough to trust myself. That isn’t fear of being wrong; it’s respect for what I don’t yet know.

Physics and astronomy make that respect feel necessary. The Drake equation, for example, doesn’t give you a clean answer about extraterrestrial life—it gives you a map of uncertainty. With only one example of life, certainty isn’t available. But better questions are. I like being in fields where the unknown isn’t embarrassing; it’s the point.

Constraints aren’t only theoretical. Sometimes they’re personal.

In second trimester of Calculus BC, I studied hard but still fell short of an A. My first instinct was to push harder. Instead, I tried to debug the system. I realized I was reviewing only after I got problems wrong. So I changed the structure: I reviewed more thoroughly while I was learning, before mistakes could settle into habits. The improvement later wasn’t just about a grade—it was evidence that iteration works across domains.

Sometimes constraints are institutional.

In physics club, when faculty opposed competitions during school hours, the easiest response would have been to accept the no. Instead, we worked around it by contacting a separate after-school organization. It wasn’t dramatic; it was practical. But it taught me that obstacles often come with hidden degrees of freedom.

And sometimes the constraints are human—different minds, different needs.

Coaching middle school Quizbowl, I’ve learned that teaching isn’t one-size-fits-all, especially when I work one-on-one with neurodivergent kids. It’s hard to generalize a single method. So I adapt—different examples, different pacing—until the student can feel progress. I still think in systems, but I’ve learned that the best systems are humane ones.

In college, I want more opportunities to live inside this loop: build structures that hold, stay honest about what I don’t know yet, and keep moving forward by designing within constraints. I want difficult classes—especially in astrophysics and particle physics—that don’t punish uncertainty but sharpen it into inquiry. I want research where the goal isn’t to sound certain, but to become accurate. And I want communities where collaboration looks like “coverage”: people coordinating, revising, and filling gaps for each other.

A buzzer moment is satisfying. But the thing I’m really chasing is the quieter satisfaction of a structure that holds—because it was built with humility, and improved by working with constraints instead of wishing them away.
