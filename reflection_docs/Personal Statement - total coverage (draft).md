# Personal Statement Draft ("total coverage" lens)

## Draft v0.2

The moment that made Quizbowl make sense to me wasn’t a buzzer question. It was the whiteboard.

If you only watch a match, it looks like the goal is to know more facts than the other team. But the real work happens earlier, when we map the terrain—history, literature, science—and decide how to become something larger than any one person. Someone takes European history. Someone takes biology. Someone else takes literature. The goal isn’t to be individually impressive; it’s to build a system we can rely on.

We call it “total coverage.” In the ideal version, no question lands in empty space.

That idea—designing a structure where the whole is resilient—keeps showing up in the projects that feel most like me.

When I started composing a piano concerto, I assumed I was writing a single line: the melody. I quickly learned I was actually building an ecosystem. A phrase that feels complete under my fingers can become thin once I imagine an orchestra behind it. If the strings are carrying the harmony, how does the flute respond without disappearing? If I add clarinets for warmth, what changes when the oboes take over later? The sound I want isn’t located in any one instrument; it emerges from the relationships.

I work the way our team plans coverage: piece by piece, over time. I begin with a main melody, orchestrate a short section, and stop. The next day I return and extend it—an introduction, a development, a conclusion—until separate ideas start behaving like one piece. Then I revise. I draft, listen, edit, and sometimes move an entire passage because it belongs somewhere else. When the structure finally holds, the music feels like it can breathe.

Even my academic interests turn into “coverage problems” in a good way. When I wrote a research paper on quantum computing, I couldn’t learn what I needed in one day. Progress came from reading across multiple days, narrowing focus, and building toward a research goal. I’ve learned that I do my best thinking when I earn a hypothesis—when I understand enough to trust my own guesses.

Coverage is also about people.

Coaching middle school Quizbowl, I’ve seen how quickly “one method” breaks. Working one-on-one with neurodivergent kids, it’s hard to generalize a single approach. So I adapt—different examples, different pacing—until the student can feel progress. That experience has made me value systems that are humane, not just efficient.

What I want from college is an environment where building systems is both necessary and celebrated. I want difficult classes I’m genuinely excited about, especially astrophysics and particle physics, and I want research that asks big questions without pretending the answers are easy. I want communities where collaboration isn’t an afterthought, but a design principle—where people coordinate, revise, and fill gaps for each other.

A buzzer moment is satisfying. But what I’m really chasing is the quieter satisfaction of a structure that holds: an orchestra that balances, a team that covers the board, a project that becomes reliable because its parts were designed to work together.
