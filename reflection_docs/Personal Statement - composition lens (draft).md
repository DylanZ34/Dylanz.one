# Personal Statement Draft (composition lens)

**Core idea:** *Seeking structure, and playing with limitations.*

## Working outline

1. **Hook (in the score):** composing a piano concerto → the moment you notice that “freedom” comes from constraints: instrumentation, voicing, balance.
2. **What you actually do:** start with a main melody; orchestrate a short section; return the next day; build introduction / development / conclusion; revise drafts; move parts around.
3. **The pattern you keep finding:** you like systems where you can build structure over time—games with progression and planning; research that narrows from rough direction into a hypothesis; learning that reveals the unknown.
4. **How you respond to limits:** when you hit a wall you iterate; example from Calc BC study habits changing trimester-to-trimester.
5. **What this means in community:** you like collaborative “coverage” systems (Quizbowl teamwork; building shared stories in Minecraft; band community).
6. **Why college (forward-looking):** you want hard classes you’re interested in (astrophysics/particle physics); you want research about the universe; you want communities that build things together.

## Draft v0.1

When I started composing a piano concerto, I assumed the hardest part would be writing a beautiful melody. I was wrong. The real challenge is learning how to give that melody structure strong enough to carry it—and realizing how much freedom exists inside constraints.

A concerto forces you to think in layers. A phrase that sounds complete under my fingers can feel thin once I imagine it in a hall, so I start asking questions that feel less like art and more like engineering: Which instrument should introduce the idea? If the strings are carrying the harmony, can a flute answer without disappearing? When I add clarinets for warmth, what happens when the oboes take over later? Composing isn’t just choosing notes; it’s arranging interactions. The sound I want isn’t located in any single line; it emerges from the way parts weave together.

That weaving is slow, and I like it that way. Most days I begin with a main melody, orchestrate a short section, and stop. The next day I return and extend it, building an introduction, development, and conclusion until separate ideas start to behave like one piece. Then the most honest part begins: revision. I draft, listen, revise, and sometimes move an entire passage to a different moment in the concerto because it belongs somewhere else. Watching rough material become something that finally sounds right is addictive.

I didn’t realize at first that I was practicing a habit that shows up everywhere else in my life: seeking structure, then playing with the limitations inside it.

I feel the same pull in the games I enjoy. I’m drawn to simulation- and story-based games because they let me create a plan and follow it through an entire world. In Civilization, I build a narrative out of turns. In Minecraft, my friends and I build a shared story over multiple days. Even when time is scarce, I keep a mental outline of what comes next, like returning to a score after a day away.

I also recognize the pattern in research. When I worked on a paper about quantum computing, progress came from spacing out reading and narrowing my focus until I could form a hypothesis I trusted. In physics and astronomy, the boundaries are part of the appeal: models are powerful because they simplify, and theories feel complete only until we learn more. Limits aren’t discouraging; they are invitations to refine the frame.

And when the frame fails, I revise. In second trimester of Calculus BC, I studied hard but still fell short. The turning point wasn’t simply trying more; it was changing how I practiced. I had been waiting until I got problems wrong to review them. Later I reviewed more thoroughly while I was learning, before I had the chance to fail. The result wasn’t just a better grade; it was proof that iteration works across domains. In music, you don’t fix a passage by playing it once and hoping. You slow down, isolate what is unstable, and rebuild.

What I’m building isn’t only individual. Some of my favorite structures are shared ones. In Quizbowl, teamwork isn’t just what happens when someone buzzes; it’s how we divide subjects, communicate what we study, and aim for total coverage so that any question has a home.

In college, I want more opportunities to do exactly this: take difficult classes I’m genuinely interested in, especially astrophysics and particle physics, while joining communities that build, revise, and improve together. I want to keep composing, keep learning, and keep chasing the moments where a set of constraints stops feeling like a wall and starts feeling like a design space.

Because the best part of any project is finding the structure, and then seeing what new possibilities appear once you commit to it.
