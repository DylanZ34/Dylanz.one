# Personal Statement Draft (epistemic humility lens)

I don—t trust my first guess.

But when I looked at it through the Drake equation, the conversation changed. With only one example of life, certainty isn—t available. What is available is a better habit: respect the unknown, and let that humility sharpen the questions.

That habit shows up in how I learn.

The more I learned, the more I could see the edge of what I didn—t understand yet—and that was progress, not failure. When I started working on a piano concerto, I assumed the melody was the point. Then orchestration made the real problem visible: how parts interact. A phrase that feels complete on piano can become thin once you imagine it in a hall. So I ask questions that feel almost scientific: Which instrument should introduce the idea? If the strings carry the harmony, can a flute answer without disappearing? If I add clarinets for warmth, what changes when the oboes take over later? The sound I want isn—t located in a single line; it emerges from relationships.

Composing also trains intellectual honesty. I draft, listen, revise, and sometimes move an entire passage to a different part of the concerto because it belongs somewhere else. What helps one student may confuse another, so I adjust—the explanation, the pacing, the practice—until the student can feel what understanding actually is.

I—ve had to apply the same approach to myself. In second trimester of Calculus BC, I studied hard but fell short of an A. Instead of treating that as a verdict, I treated it as feedback. In college, I want to be in places where this mindset is normal. I want research where the goal isn—t to sound certain, but to become accurate. I still have instincts. But the way I move forward is by earning them—learning enough to trust the next step, and changing it when new understanding arrives.
