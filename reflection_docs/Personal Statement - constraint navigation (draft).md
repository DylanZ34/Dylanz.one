# Personal Statement Draft (constraint-navigation lens)

## Draft v0.2

I used to think setbacks were signs to stop.

Now I treat them like design constraints.

The clearest example is music. When I started composing a piano concerto, I imagined inspiration as something that arrives fully formed—a beautiful melody, a clean ending, a piece that simply works. Reality is less cinematic and more interesting. A concerto forces you to choose limits on purpose: which instruments exist, how they balance, how many ideas can fit before the listener gets lost. And once you commit to those limits, you have to build inside them.

Most days I begin with a main melody, orchestrate a short section, and stop. The next day I return and add the next part. Over multiple days, I build an introduction, a development, and a conclusion until the piece starts to hold together. Then I revise. I draft, listen, edit, and sometimes move an entire passage because what worked as an opening doesn—t work once the orchestra is full. The constraint changes, so the design has to change.

At some point I realized I wasn—t just composing music. I was practicing a way of responding to the world: when the path is blocked, redesign the path.

I saw that in school.

In second trimester of Calculus BC, I worked hard and still fell short of an A. My first reaction was frustration—the feeling of pushing against a wall. But effort without structure is just noise. So I debugged my process. I realized I was reviewing only after I got problems wrong. I changed the constraint by changing my system: I reviewed more thoroughly while I was learning, before mistakes could take root. What improved later wasn—t just a grade; it was my confidence that I could change outcomes by changing method.

Sometimes the constraint is institutional.

In physics club, when faculty opposed having competitions during school hours, the easy response would have been to accept the no. Instead, we worked around the blockage by contacting a separate after-school organization. The workaround wasn—t dramatic, but it taught me something I—ve held onto: obstacles often come with hidden degrees of freedom. If one channel is closed, you look for another.

Constraints also show up as differences between people.

Coaching middle school Quizbowl, I—ve learned there is no universal method, especially when I—m working one-on-one with neurodivergent kids. It—s hard to generalize one specific way to teach. So I adjust—examples, pacing, practice—until the student can feel momentum. The goal isn—t to force someone into my preferred structure; it—s to build a structure that fits them.

Even my research interests are driven by constraints in a way that excites me. When I wrote a research paper on quantum computing, I couldn—t learn what I needed in one day. The constraint of time forced a better approach: read across multiple days, narrow focus, and build toward a hypothesis only after I understood enough to trust my own guesses. In physics and astronomy, the constraints are deeper: we have incomplete models and limited observations, yet we still try to learn about the universe and our place in it.

In college, I want a place where constraints are treated as raw material. I want to study astrophysics and particle physics and do research that asks ambitious questions while respecting uncertainty. I want communities that build systems that get stronger because members design around limitations rather than pretending they don—t exist. And I want to keep composing, because it is my favorite practice of all: committing to constraints so fully that they stop feeling like walls and start feeling like a space to create.

Setbacks still happen. But now, when something blocks the path, my first question is no longer —Why me?— It—s: what are the constraints, and what can I redesign?
